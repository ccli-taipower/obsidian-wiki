Are you ever mystified when it comes to choosing fingerings for a new piece? Indeed, the topic of fingering is a confusing one for nearly all piano students, especially once the pieces start to move away from simple five-finger positions.

Many beginning pianists are full of questions that often go unanswered: How do piano teachers know which fingerings to use? Do I have to use the fingerings suggested in the score, or am I free to change them? Do professional pianists all use the same fingerings?

In this article, I’ll give you some of the important tips and considerations when it comes to choosing piano fingerings.

### Tip #1: Move the fingers as little as possible

Each time you have to jump to a new hand position, there’s the potential for something to go wrong. Look for fingerings that allow you to play groups of notes in one single hand position without moving. This makes your playing much more secure, and usually makes the pieces easier to play.

### Tip #2: Feel free to change the fingerings indicated if you find something better.

I’ll let you on in a secret- I almost never play fingerings exactly the way they’re indicated in the music. Remember that the given fingerings are merely suggestions. You are under absolutely no obligation to follow them if you find the given fingerings strange!

Particularly in older scores, editors often added very strange fingerings. For example, many editors seem to have had the view that one should never play a repeated note with the same finger, no matter the tempo or character of the music. There are all sorts of cases where I find the suggested fingerings absolutely bizarre, so feel free to go your own way if you think so too!

### Tip #3: For rapid passages, avoid weak finger combinations.

If you need to play quickly, try to avoid finger combinations like 4-5 and 3-4-5. For example, given the choice, I would much rather play the following group of notes with the fingers 1-2-3 and not 3-4-5:

![piano fingerings 1](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201024%20281'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

The reason for this is simple. Even though pianists do everything possible to train the weaker fingers, we still have better power and control with the stronger ones due simply to the physiognomy of the human hand. Some pianists like to have practice fingerings that train the weak fingers, but switch to stronger fingers for performances.

### Tip #4: Choose fingerings that emphasize the articulation.

Let’s take a look at an example. The first fingering here is much better than the second one:

![repeated fingering](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20994%20408'%3E%3C/svg%3E) ![repeated fingering 2](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%20994%20408'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

Even though the second fingering requires less jumping, the first fingering helps us to automatically play the right articulation. The fact that we have to jump after every second note is a good thing, because the slur tells us that we should make every second note short. Of course it’s good to stay in the same hand position when possible, but it’s often even better to play certain phrases with a fingering that forces you to do the proper articulation.

### Tip #5: Stick to the same fingerings for repeated passages.

Music is full of repeated phrases and sections. If you’re using different fingerings each time, you’re making life unnecessarily difficult. Try to be as consistent as possible. A similar example of this principle involves [sequences](https://www.musictheoryacademy.com/composing-music/sequences/) – short groups of notes that repeat each time higher or lower, like this one:

![fingering for sequences](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201024%20297'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

In this example, I’ve chosen to repeat the fingers 5-4-1-2 because we have essentially the same four notes four times in a row, with all the same intervals, just shifted a step lower each time. Not only is it easier to play when we repeat the same four fingers, but it also helps us to give each group of four notes the exact same emphasis.

### Tip #6: Don’t cross fingers over a higher-numbered finger.

I see beginners do this all the time, and in most all cases it’s *really* bad. It looks something like this:

![piano fingering example](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%201024%20461'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

See how I’m crossing the third finger over the fourth finger? Don’t do that!

Don’t cross 2 over 3, 3 over 4, or 4 over 5. Instead, plan ahead and put the thumb under. It’s usually much easier to play this way, and it prevents you from needing to twist the hand and wrist to the left or right. Likewise, don’t put 5 under 4, 4 under 3, or 3 under 2.There are a few exceptions to this rule, particularly in more advanced piano music, but even then it’s quite rare. There’s usually a better solution.

### Tip #8: Use the standard fingerings for scales and chords.

There’s a good reason we [practice scales](https://petersonpianoacademy.com/why-learn-scales/), arpeggios and chords. They’re found everywhere in piano music, and if you learn the fingerings in advance, you can save a ton of practice time and also become a much better sight reader.

### Tip #9: Write your fingerings down! (But only as few as needed.)

Many beginners tend to use whatever fingers happen to be convenient in the moment. This means that their fingering is completely inconsistent; each time they play the piece, the fingering is a little different. This also makes the piece much more difficult to learn, as the brain never gets the repetition it needs to make the piece more secure in the memory.

That’s why I absolutely recommend that you write your fingerings down. There’s no need to write a fingering for every single note– just the places where something important happens, like the thumb going under. This way, you can be sure to play the piece with the same fingering each time. You’ll be surprised how much faster you’ll learn pieces when the fingering stays consistent.

### Tip #10: Experiment!

Even professionals don’t always get it right the first time. Finding good piano fingerings requires plenty of trial and error. Try different things and see which fingerings work best for you. And keep in mind that an optimal fingering will also depend somewhat on the size and shape of your hand, which means that ultimately only *you* can decide which fingering is best!
