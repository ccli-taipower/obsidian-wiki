In [music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music "Music"), **fingering**, or on stringed instruments sometimes also called **stopping**, is the choice of which and hand [positions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_\(string_technique\) "Position (string technique)") to use when playing certain [musical instruments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_instrument "Musical instrument"). Fingering typically changes throughout a [piece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piece_\(music\) "Piece (music)"); the challenge of choosing good fingering for a [piece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_composition "Musical composition") is to make the hand movements as comfortable as possible without changing hand position too often. A fingering can be the result of the working process of the [composer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composer "Composer"), who puts it into the manuscript, an [editor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Editor "Editor"), who adds it into the printed score, or the [performer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Performer "Performer"), who puts his or her own fingering in the score or in performance.

> *Fingering*... also *stopping*... (1) A system of symbols (usually [Arabic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_numbers "Arabic numbers")) for the fingers of the hand (or some subset of them) used to associate specific notes with specific fingers.... (2) Control of finger movements and position to achieve physiological efficiency, acoustical accuracy \[frequency and amplitude\] (or effect) and [musical articulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_articulation "Musical articulation").[^5]<sup><span title="Pages: 314–315">: 314–315</span></sup>

A *substitute fingering* is an alternative to the indicated fingering, not to be confused with a [finger substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finger_substitution "Finger substitution"). Depending on the instrument, not all the fingers may be used. For example, [saxophonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saxophone "Saxophone") do not use the right thumb, [bowed instruments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowed_instrument "Bowed instrument") (usually) only use the fingers and not the thumbs, and [harpists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpist "Harpist") pluck with every digit except the little finger.

## Brass instruments

Fingering applies to the [rotary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotary_valve#Use_in_brass_instruments "Rotary valve") and [piston valves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piston_valve#Brass_instruments "Piston valve") employed on many brass instruments.

The [trombone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trombone "Trombone"), a fully [chromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatonic_and_chromatic "Diatonic and chromatic") brass instrument without [valves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brass_instrument_valve "Brass instrument valve"), employs equivalent numbered notation for slide positions rather than fingering.

## Keyboard instruments

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Clementi1801Fingering.png/250px-Clementi1801Fingering.png)

English fingering · zero variant

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Hummel1827Fingering.png/250px-Hummel1827Fingering.png)

English fingering · cross variant

In notation for [keyboard instruments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_keyboard "Musical keyboard"), numbers are used to relate to the fingers themselves, not the hand position on the keyboard. In modern scores, the fingers are numbered from 1 to 5 on each hand: the thumb is 1, the index finger is 2, the middle finger is 3, the ring finger is 4 and the little finger is 5.

Earlier usage varied by region. In Britain in the 19th century, the thumb was shown by a cross (+) or number 0 and the fingers were numbered from 1 to 4. This was known as "English fingering" while the other way (from 1 to 5) was known as "continental fingering". However, from the beginning of the 20th century the British adopted the continental (1 to 5) fingering, which remains in use everywhere.[^6]

### Piano

After [Cristofori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartolomeo_Cristofori "Bartolomeo Cristofori") invented the [hammered-string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammer_dulcimer "Hammer dulcimer") [pianoforte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pianoforte "Pianoforte") from the [plucked-string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harp "Harp") [harpsichord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpsichord "Harpsichord") in 1700, and after it became popular in the decades after 1740, eventually replacing the harpsichord, the piano technique developed tremendously (it was parallel with the piano builders´ progress and piano pedagogy, and as part of it piano fingering changed).

There are only a few publications about piano fingering:

- [Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Philipp_Emanuel_Bach "Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach") [^1] dedicated several paragraphs to fingering for keyboards in his 1762 book on playing the [clavier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clavichord "Clavichord").[^7]
- The British pianist [Tobias Matthay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobias_Matthay "Tobias Matthay") wrote a 1908 pamphlet on fingering.[^8]
- [Julien Musafia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julien_Musafia "Julien Musafia") published a book in 1971 that includes fingering examples, mostly drawn from the Beethoven's Violin and [Piano Sonatas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piano_sonatas_\(Beethoven\) "Piano sonatas (Beethoven)") and from the *Preludes and Fugues* of [Shostakovich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich "Dmitri Shostakovich").[^9]
- [Rami Bar-Niv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rami_Bar-Niv "Rami Bar-Niv") published a book in 2012 that teaches piano fingering.[^10] [^11]

### History

[Johann Sebastian Bach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach "Johann Sebastian Bach") introduced an innovation in fingering for the organ and the [clavier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clavichord "Clavichord").[^2] Prior to Bach, playing rarely involved the thumb. Bach's new fingering retained many features of the conventional fingering up until that point, including the passing of one finger under or over another [^3] but introduced the far greater use of the thumb. Modern fingering also uses the thumb to a similar extent, and involves the passing of the thumb under the other fingers, but does not, as Bach's did, generally involve the passing of any other fingers over or under one another.[^12]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Cello_first_position.svg/250px-Cello_first_position.svg.png)

Cello first position fingerings

In the 1980s [Lindley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Lindley "Mark Lindley") and Boxall showed that the above relies solely on C.P.E. Bach's testimony: All the extant fingerings from J.S. Bach and his circle use the ancient methods, with very limited use of the thumb. More recently it has been shown that all his harpsichord works and most of the organ works as well are playable with the old technique.[^13]

## Bowed string instruments

On string instruments fingers are numbered from 1 to 4, beginning with the index finger, the thumb not being counted because it does not normally play on a string, and '0' indicating an [open string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_string_\(music\) "Open string (music)"). In those cases on string instruments where the thumb *is* used (such as high notes on a cello in the special [thumb position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thumb_position "Thumb position") "), it is represented by a symbol the shape of an 'O' with a vertical stem below (somewhat similar to ' [Ǫ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C7%AA "Ǫ") ' or [ϙ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koppa_\(letter\) "Koppa (letter)"), for instance).[^14] [Guitar music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guitar "Guitar") indicates thumb, occasionally used to finger [bass notes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bass_note "Bass note") on the low E string, with a 'T'. [Position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_\(string_technique\) "Position (string technique)") may be indicated through [ordinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number_\(linguistics\) "Ordinal number (linguistics)") (e.g., "third" as opposed to "three") or (uncommon) [Roman numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numeral "Roman numeral"). A string may also be indicated through Roman numerals, often I-IV, or by its open-string note. A change in positions is referred to as a [shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_\(string_technique\) "Shift (string technique)"). Guitar music indicates position with Roman numerals and string designations with circled numbers.

## Plucked string instruments

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/GuitarFingeringScore.png/250px-GuitarFingeringScore.png)

Fingered music for guitar: the numbers 1 to 4 indicate the stopping fingers, 0 an open note, circled numbers strings, and dashed numbers slipping

The [classical guitar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_guitar "Classical guitar") also has a fingering notation system for the [plucking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_guitar#Plucking_of_the_string "Classical guitar") hand, known as *pima* (or less commonly *pimac*), abbreviations of Spanish; where ***p*** = *pulgar* (thumb), ***i*** = *índice* (index finger), ***m*** = *medio* (middle finger), ***a*** = *anular* (ring finger) and, very rarely, ***c*** = *chico* (little finger).[^15] It is usually only notated in scores where a passage is particularly difficult, or requires specific fingering for the plucking hand. Otherwise, plucking-hand fingering is generally left to the discretion of the guitarist.

## Woodwind instruments

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/SaxophonefingeringchartHorizontal.png/500px-SaxophonefingeringchartHorizontal.png)

Saxophone fingerings for normal range

Fingering of woodwind instruments is not always simple or intuitive, depending on how the [acoustic impedance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustic_impedance "Acoustic impedance") of the bore is affected by the distribution and size of apertures along its length, leading to the formation of [standing waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_wave "Standing wave") at the desired pitch. Several alternate fingerings may exist for any given pitch.

Simple [flutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flute "Flute") (including [recorders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recorder_\(musical_instrument\) "Recorder (musical instrument)")) as well as [bagpipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bagpipe "Bagpipe") [chanters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chanter "Chanter") have [open holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_hole "Tone hole") which are closed by the pads of the player's fingertips. Some such instruments use simple keywork to extend the player's reach for one or two notes. The keywork on instruments such as modern [flutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_concert_flute "Western concert flute"), [clarinets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clarinet "Clarinet"), or [oboes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oboe "Oboe") is elaborate and variable. Modern flutes typically use the [Boehm system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boehm_system "Boehm system") of keywork, while clarinets typically use a [similarly named system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boehm_system_\(clarinet\) "Boehm system (clarinet)") invented by [Hyacinthe Klosé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyacinthe_Klos%C3%A9 "Hyacinthe Klosé"). In Germany and Austria a different system of clarinet keywork, the [Öhler system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%96hler_system "Öhler system"), is most used.

## Cross-fingering

*Cross-fingering* is any fingering, "requiring a closed hole or holes below an open one".[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 228">: 228</span></sup>

"Opening successive tone holes in woodwind instruments shortens the standing wave in the bore. However, the standing wave propagates past the first open hole, so its frequency can be affected by closing other tone holes further downstream. This is called cross fingering, and in some instruments is used to produce the 'sharps and flats' missing from their natural scales." [^16]

In the Baroque period cross-fingering improved, allowing music in an increasing variety of keys, but in the Classical and Romantic periods flute design changes – particularly larger tone holes – made cross-fingering less practical, while mechanical keywork increasingly provided an easy alternative to playing chromatic notes without cross-fingerings.[^17] The Boehm system was developed in part to replace cross-fingerings.[^5] The first key added to the flute, the short F key,[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 228">: 228</span> </sup> crossed the flute's body, replacing a fingering with an open hole above a closed one, and is presumably the origin of the name "cross" for such crossfingerings.

*Fork fingering* is any fingering where a central hole is uncovered while the holes to each side are kept covered. One advantage of the [Giorgi flute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giorgi_flute "Giorgi flute") was that it removed the necessity of fork fingering for playing chromatic notes.[^5]<sup><span title="Page: 352">: 352</span></sup>

## False fingering and alternate fingering

The term *false fingering* is used in instruments such as woodwinds, brass, and stringed instruments where different fingerings can produce the same note, but where the [timbre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timbre "Timbre") or tone quality is distinctly different from each other. If the tone quality is not distinctly different between the two notes, the term *alternate fingering* is often used instead.

When the note is played in such a way as to draw the distinction from the expected tone quality [^4] it is often called a *false fingering*. The technique is common in [jazz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jazz "Jazz") music, especially on wind instruments such as the saxophone.[^18]

On a guitar, for example, the same pitch can be played on a heavier, over-wound string, rather than a plain single-wire string (solid wire string). The note played on the heavier string will sound significantly different from one played on a single wire string, so playing the same pitch on differently made strings in short succession can accentuate the different tone colors without actually changing the pitch.
