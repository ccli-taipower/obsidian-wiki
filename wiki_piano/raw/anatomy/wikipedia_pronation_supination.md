## Forearm Pronation and Supination

Pronation at the forearm is a rotational movement where the hand and upper arm are turned so the **thumbs point towards the body**. In supination, the thumbs point away from the body.

- **Supinated**: palm faces anteriorly (standard anatomical position)
- **Pronated**: palm faces posteriorly
- **Neutral**: palm faces medially (thumbs up, as when holding a hammer)

## Anatomical Mechanics

- Rotation occurs at **two joints**: the proximal radioulnar joint (near elbow) and the distal radioulnar joint (near wrist)
- The **radius rotates around the ulna** (not the other way around) — ulna stays fixed as the axis
- **Supination** requires a dorsal glide of the distal radius
- **Pronation** requires a palmar glide of the distal radius
- As the forearm moves from pronation to supination, **interosseous membrane fibers change from relaxed to tense** at the neutral position

## Muscles

| Muscle | Action | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Biceps brachii** | Major supinator (especially when elbow is flexed) | Primary under heavy load |
| **Supinator** | Supinates forearm | Most active in unresisted supination; works in all elbow positions |
| **Pronator teres** | Major pronator, weak elbow flexor | Compression causes pronator teres syndrome |
| **Pronator quadratus** | Pronates forearm | Deep, near wrist |
| **Brachioradialis** | Returns forearm to neutral (from extremes) | Not a true pronator or supinator |

# Supinator Muscle (detailed)

**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supinator_muscle

- **Origin**: lateral epicondyle of humerus, supinator crest of ulna, radial collateral ligament, annular ligament of radius
- **Insertion**: lateral proximal radial shaft; superficial fibers to radial tuberosity and oblique line; deeper fibers wrap around the radius's neck
- **Innervation**: deep division of radial nerve (C5–C7, primarily C6)
- **Action**: brings the hand into the supinated position; functions in all elbow positions
- **Force**: disabling the supinator reduces supination strength by 64%
- **Activity**: most active muscle in unresisted forearm supination; biceps takes over under heavy load
- **Clinical**: compression causes supinator entrapment syndrome — may paralyze extensor muscles

# Pronator Teres Muscle (detailed)

**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronator_teres_muscle

- **Origin (two heads)**:
  - Humeral head: medial supracondylar ridge / medial epicondyle via common flexor tendon
  - Ulnar head: medial side of coronoid process of ulna
- **Insertion**: rough impression at middle of lateral radius
- **Innervation**: median nerve (C6–C7)
- **Actions**:
  1. Pronates the forearm (primary)
  2. Weakly flexes the elbow (accessory)
- **Clinical**: **pronator teres syndrome** — compression of median nerve causes neurogenic pain, numbness in median nerve distribution with repetitive forearm pronation/supination

# Wrist Anatomical Terms

**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_motion (wrist section)

## Radial / Ulnar Deviation

- **Adduction = ulnar deviation**: hand moves toward the ulnar styloid (toward the little finger / f5)
- **Abduction = radial deviation**: hand moves toward the radial styloid (toward the thumb / f1)

## Flexion / Extension

- **Palmarflexion**: flexion of the hand at the wrist (toward palm / ventral)
- **Dorsiflexion**: extension of the hand at the wrist (toward dorsum / back of hand)

# Piano-relevance Notes (Claude annotation, 2026-04-11)

Key extractions for the piano fingering DP:

1. **The rotation axis is the ulna** (f5 side). This means small-finger-side is the "anchor" and thumb-side is the "mobile" end. DP consequence: f5 as pivot makes physical sense; rotation around f5 is cheaper than rotation around f1.

2. **Supinator reduces strength by 64% when disabled** — supination is a dedicated, strong movement. Fast-alternating supination/pronation (trill, Alberti bass) is not trivial and needs careful muscle recruitment.

3. **Pronator teres syndrome** is a real RSI — repetitive forearm pronation/supination causes median nerve compression. Long-term rotation overuse = injury. This validates "rotation should be minimized in magnitude, not just count."

4. **Biceps takes over under heavy load** — ff octaves / chords use biceps supination, not supinator alone. Dynamic-dependent biomechanics.

5. **Ulnar deviation + palmarflexion** = classical carpal tunnel posture. DP consequence: penalize fingerings that force both simultaneously, even if each is individually OK.
